Female, 41 years old.
Smoker.
Psychiatricly impaired.
He came to the emergency room for a fifth time in 12 days due to intense and continuous holocraneal headache that did not present habitual analgesia together with vomiting.
No fever.
No other symptoms.
On physical examination she only showed thinness, all the rest including neurological examination was normal (Glasgow, cranial nerves, strength, sensitivity and meningeal signs).
Afebrile and hemodynamically stable.
Urgently, general blood tests were performed with results of blood count, biochemistry and coagulation within normal parameters, as well as normal cranial CT.
With this, it was decided to perform lumbar puncture for microbiological analysis (culture and PCR) as well as cytobiochemical (438 leukocytes: 1% polymorphonuclear, 99% mononuclear).

During admission chest X-ray was normal and serology for HIV, CMV, EBV and VZV was requested. The results were negative; PCR of cerebrospinal fluid herpes simplex 1, 2 and 6, mycobacterial, VV was also negative.
Finally, blood samples were also taken for virus serology, being positive for Parotiditis Virus with a strongly positive IgM and negative IgG, compatible with acute process.
With this result, the patient is diagnosed with▁lymphocytic meningitis due to parotitis virus recognizing the patient having had contact with her child who had suffered an acute mesotheliitis for a month.
The clinical evolution of the patient was excellent with symptomatic treatment.
Currently asymptomatic.
The importance of this case lies in the ability to recognize cases of meningitis that occur more frequently, although it may be related to germs that less frequently cause acute meningitis, as in our case, due to Parot virus.
