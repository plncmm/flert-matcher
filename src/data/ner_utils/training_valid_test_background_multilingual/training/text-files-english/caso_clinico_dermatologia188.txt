Anamnesis A 19-year-old male, born in Morocco, who came to our emergency department with generalized asymptomatic skin lesions for two months, who had recently travelled to the emergency department with cutaneous ulcers.
The anamnesis included fever and other symptoms.
She had no personal history of interest and did not take any usual treatment.
He was single, lived with three apparently healthy cats in his home, but had no other family affected.
He commented that an antifungal cream had been applied without improvement.
Six months ago, she was diagnosed with onycomycosis due to R. rubrum in her right fingernail. She was treated with oral and topical antifungal medication for 1 month with poor response.
Physical examination revealed nodular plaques of variable size, erythematous, well-defined, active, raised and scaly edges with central clearance located in the right frontal abdomen.
On the first finger of the right hand, he presented distal onychodystrophy and coloration of the nail plate as well as onycholysis.
Additional features A sample of the scabs of the abdominal lesion was collected with a mocket for fungal culture.
Diagnosis Given the clinical and microbiological findings, a diagnosis of tinea corporis secondary to nail onychomycosis of the first finger of the right hand was made.
The culture at one month was positive for Tricho rubrum.
Treatment Treatment Treatment was initiated with oral terbinafine 250 mg every 24 hours for at least 3 months for nail and body involvement and topical Miconazole twice daily for 4 weeks for body lesions.
Sepsis was performed 4 weeks after starting treatment.
She came to the outpatient clinic for 6 weeks of follow-up consultation due to congenital malformation, and cutaneous lesions had developed and the nail plate had grown healthy.
The analytical results showed no relevant findings.
No new recurrences were observed during follow-up.
