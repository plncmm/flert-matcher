A 59-year-old male patient with a history of Diabetes Mellitus II diagnosed one year ago.
The patient was seen in the emergency department of our hospital with a history of progressive general▁malaise in the last 3 months, weight loss, and night sweats.
The week prior to admission, the patient had pain in the left iliac fossa and flank, fever up to 38.5 C, and few vomiting.
On physical examination, it was observed that the patient was afflicted with coughing, pale, feverish, tachycardic, with a slight bloating in the oral cavity.
The admission diagnoses were: febrile syndrome, abdominal pain under study, consumptive syndrome, immunosuppression, oral candidiasis.
Computed tomography (CT) of abdomen showing multiple retroperitoneal lymphadenopathies and at the root level of the mesentery, some confluent, hypodense.
A peripheral impregnation was observed after contrast injection.
CT axial section showing retroperitoneal▁ganglia that are peripherally impregnated after contrast medium injection.
CT sagittal and coronal reconstruction with hypodense adenopathies,▁surrounded by peripheral impregnation of the uppermost artery (asteriscos).
Subsequently, immunosuppression associated with HIV was confirmed.
Puncture of lymph nodes was performed under CT, obtaining bacilli (+) for tuberculosis (TBC).
Biopsy shows abundant intracellular bacilli.
Histological sample obtained by one of the lymph nodes showed the presence of M. Tuberculosis.
