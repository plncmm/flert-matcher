On February 28, 2020 a firefighter was admitted to the emergency department 54 years after a crisis characterized by▁clonic movements in the right arm and loss of consciousness.
His relatives reported a sudden onset of rhythmic and involuntary movements in the arm and right hand, lasting 1 or 2 minutes; then, the patient showed stiffness and was seated with tape.
Upon▁awakening, she was disoriented for about 30 minutes.
On admission to the emergency department, she reported having conjunctivitis in the last 10 days and fever in the last week.
She had no neurological history, had been medicated with ophthalmic▁steroids during the last days, but did not take any regular medication; there was no toxic history.
Vital signs, peripheral blood oxygen saturation and ECG were normal (temperature 36.5 °C); neurological examination showed no focal absences.
Analytical tests revealed neutrophilia (85.5%, reference values 40.0), normal white blood cell count (6.38 × 1000/dL, reference values 40.0%), ALT/29 normal reference values (14.08–49 mmol/L), serum reference values × 1000), also high).
Cranial CT did not yield relevant results.
A chest X-ray showed a single dysventilated lingual tonsillar band.
Urocultivations, urine analysis and uroantagonists tests for Streptococcus pneumoniae and Legionella pneumophila were normal.
The patient's electroencephalogram obtained the next day showed no abnormality.
When Mycoplasma pneumoniae was suspected, a dose of 500 mg per day was prescribed for three days.
On February 29, the patient was discharged with a diagnosis of «first-focused motor crisis». Antiepileptic medication was not prescribed.
4 days later, on March 4, the patient was admitted again to the emergency room with cough, high fever and dyspnea; her daughter also had high fever.
His temperature was 38.5 °C, his pulse was 100 l.p.m.
peripheral blood oxygen saturation was 94%.
Analytical tests showed an increase in inflammation markers; chest X-ray showed bilateral perihilosal consolidations; high resolution CT showed bilateral ground glass opacities.
From epidemiological data and following government guidelines, two nasopharyngeal swabs were obtained, which were positive for SARS-CoV-2 by qRT-PCR.
The patient was hospitalized in the intermediate care unit and non-invasive ventilation was applied.
After two weeks of antiviral therapy (lopinavir/ritonavir), the patient fully resolved and remains seizure free.
